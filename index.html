14. References Strategy

The Reference Reality

  • Companies check references for 80% of final candidates
  • 3-5 professional references typically required
  • Often contacted before offer

Who to Ask (Priority Order)

  1. Direct supervisor/manager (most recent)
  2. Professor from major
  3. Internship/co-op supervisor
  4. Academic advisor/faculty mentor
  5. Volunteer coordinator/nonprofit supervisor
  6. Professional colleague you've worked with directly
❌ Who NOT to ask: Family or friends (unless they've directly supervised your work), anyone you haven't spoken to in 2+ years, anyone who might give a lukewarm recommendation, current supervisor (if they don't know you're searching)

How to Request a Letter of Recommendation

Request Letter Prompt for Claude

I need to request a letter of recommendation from [Name/Organization]. **CONTEXT:** - Relationship: [How you know them - supervisor, professor, volunteer coordinator] - When worked together: [Dates/Duration] - What they know you for: [Projects, achievements, skills demonstrated] - Position applying for: [Title, company, or graduate program] - Deadline: [When needed by] - Reference contact: [Their current email/organization] --- Write a professional email request that: 1. **OPENS WITH CONTEXT** - Remind them of your relationship - Mention specific project/time you worked together 2. **MAKES THE ASK CLEAR** - Specific position/program you're applying for - Why you're asking them specifically - What qualities/experiences they can speak to 3. **MAKES IT EASY** - Offer to provide: resume, job description, talking points - Give clear deadline - Explain how they'll be contacted (letter vs. phone reference) 4. **PROVIDES AN OUT** - "If this timing doesn't work, I completely understand" - Let them decline gracefully 5. **INCLUDES FOLLOW-UP INFO** - "The hiring manager may call you directly as a reference" - Provide your contact info for questions Keep tone: Respectful, appreciative, professional but warm.

Thank you again for your support!

Important Timing Tips

  • Ask 2-3 weeks before you need it (people are busy!)
  • Follow up 1 week before deadline with a friendly reminder
  • Let them know immediately when the hiring manager contacts them
  • Send thank you after they provide the reference
  • Update them on outcome - whether you got the job or not!

What If They Say No?

Don't take it personally! Common reasons:
  • Too busy with their own work
  • Company policy against references
  • Don't feel they know your work well enough
  • Haven't worked together recently enough

Response: "I completely understand! Thanks for considering it. Would you be able to suggest someone else who might be a good reference?"

Maintaining Your Reference List

  • Update annually - Keep in touch with references
  • Don't overuse - Don't have same person do 10+ references
  • Rotate strategically - Use different references for different types of roles
  • Always ask permission - Even if they've done it before
  • Show appreciation - Small thank you note, coffee, LinkedIn recommendation for them

16. Salary Negotiation Strategy

💰 The Negotiation Reality:
  • 18.8% average increase for those who negotiate
  • 55% don't try
  • 46% of ages 18-29 don't feel comfortable asking

Bottom line: Negotiation is expected. Companies build room into offers.

What to Negotiate Beyond Salary

If they can't move on salary:

  • Signing bonus (easier to approve)
  • Earlier performance review (with written salary adjustment)
  • Extra PTO (2-5 days)
  • Remote flexibility (1-2 days/week)
  • Professional development budget ($1-3K/year)
  • Relocation assistance
  • Start date flexibility

When NOT to Negotiate

Don't negotiate if:
  • Offer already at/above market for your level
  • No competing offers, minimal leverage
  • Highly competitive role with many qualified candidates
  • Company stated salary non-negotiable
  • Desperate for job, can't risk losing it
